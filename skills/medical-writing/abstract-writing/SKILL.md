# Abstract Writing

## Purpose

The abstract is the most-read part of any medical publication. It determines whether the full paper is read, cited, or selected for conference presentation. A well-crafted abstract must convey the study's essence accurately, completely, and compellingly within severe word limits.

## Structured Abstract

Most biomedical journals require structured abstracts for original research, with labeled sections that mirror the IMRAD format. Typical sections:

### Background (or Introduction / Context)
**Word budget**: 2-3 sentences (40-60 words)

**Must include**:
- The clinical or scientific context (one sentence)
- The specific knowledge gap or problem
- The study objective (the single most important sentence in the abstract)

**Template**: "[Disease/condition] affects [population/burden]. [Gap in knowledge]. We aimed to [specific objective]."

**Example**: "Community-acquired pneumonia remains a leading cause of hospitalization and mortality. Whether procalcitonin-guided antibiotic therapy reduces antibiotic exposure without compromising outcomes is uncertain. We aimed to compare procalcitonin-guided versus standard antibiotic duration in hospitalized adults with community-acquired pneumonia."

### Methods
**Word budget**: 3-5 sentences (60-100 words)

**Must include**:
- Study design (RCT, cohort, case-control, cross-sectional, systematic review)
- Setting (single center, multicenter, country, hospital type)
- Participants (who, how many, key eligibility criteria)
- Intervention/Exposure (what was compared to what)
- Primary outcome (precisely defined)
- Key analytical approach (if space permits)

**Do not include**: Excessive detail on statistical methods, secondary outcomes, subgroup analyses. Save these for the full paper.

### Results
**Word budget**: 5-8 sentences (100-150 words)

**Must include**:
- Number of participants analyzed (and enrollment numbers if space allows)
- Primary outcome result with effect estimate, confidence interval, and p-value
- Key secondary outcomes (1-2 most important)
- Absolute numbers AND rates, not just relative measures

**Template**: "[N] participants were [enrolled/analyzed]. The primary outcome occurred in [n/N (%) in group A versus n/N (%) in group B] ([effect measure] [95% CI], p = [value])."

**Principles**:
- Lead with the primary outcome
- Provide absolute and relative measures: "Mortality was 12% vs 18% (RR 0.67, 95% CI 0.48-0.92)"
- Include actual numbers, not just percentages
- Do not interpret results — save that for Conclusions

### Conclusions
**Word budget**: 1-3 sentences (30-50 words)

**Must include**:
- Direct answer to the study question (match to the objective in Background)
- Clinical or research implication (one sentence)

**Must avoid**:
- Overstating findings ("This study proves..." — use "suggests," "demonstrates," "indicates")
- Conclusions not supported by the presented results
- Introducing information not mentioned in the abstract
- Recommending practice change from a single study (unless very strong evidence)
- Excessive hedging that obscures the message

**Template**: "In [population], [intervention/exposure] was associated with [outcome effect]. These findings suggest that [clinical implication]."

### Additional Structured Sections (Journal-Dependent)
- **Trial registration**: Required for RCTs. Include registry name and number.
- **Funding**: Some journals include funding source in the abstract.
- **Keywords**: Typically 3-6 MeSH terms or free-text keywords listed after the abstract.

## Unstructured Abstract

Used by some journals (especially in the humanities, basic sciences, and some general medicine journals like NEJM). A single paragraph without section headers.

### Structure (Implicit)
Even without headers, the unstructured abstract should follow the same logical flow:
1. Context and gap (1-2 sentences)
2. Objective (1 sentence)
3. Methods (2-3 sentences)
4. Key results (3-5 sentences)
5. Conclusion (1-2 sentences)

### Special Considerations
- Requires tighter, more integrated writing (no section headers to guide the reader)
- Transition words become important for flow
- Easier to create a narrative feel, but harder to ensure completeness

## Word Limits

| Abstract Type | Typical Limit |
|---|---|
| Original research (structured) | 250-350 words |
| Systematic review (structured) | 300-400 words |
| Conference abstract | 250-300 words |
| Case report | 150-250 words |
| Unstructured | 150-250 words |

### Strategies for Meeting Word Limits
1. **Cut adjectives and adverbs**: "Significantly decreased" → "Decreased." The confidence interval tells the reader about significance.
2. **Remove filler phrases**: "It is well established that" → delete. "The purpose of this study was to investigate" → "We investigated"
3. **Use active voice**: "The intervention was administered by physicians" → "Physicians administered the intervention" (saves words and improves clarity)
4. **Combine sentences**: Two short sentences conveying related information → one sentence with a conjunction or semicolon
5. **Remove redundancy**: If the objective states you studied "adults with diabetes," do not repeat this in Methods
6. **Use numerals**: "Twenty-five" → "25" (saves characters/words)
7. **Prioritize ruthlessly**: Include primary outcome. Include 1-2 key secondary outcomes at most. Everything else goes in the full paper.

## Key Elements Editors Look For

### During Initial Editorial Review
Editors spend 30-60 seconds on most abstracts. They look for:

1. **Clear, important research question**: Does the study address a clinically meaningful gap?
2. **Appropriate study design**: Is the design capable of answering the question? (RCT for efficacy, cohort for risk factors, etc.)
3. **Adequate sample size**: Is N reasonable for the study design and expected effect size?
4. **Clear primary outcome**: Is it stated? Is it clinically meaningful?
5. **Effect estimate with CI**: Not just p-values. Confidence intervals convey precision and clinical relevance.
6. **Conclusion that matches results**: Overstated conclusions are an immediate red flag.
7. **Novelty**: Does this add meaningfully to existing evidence?

### Red Flags That Trigger Desk Rejection
- No clear research question or objective
- Inappropriate study design for the question asked
- Missing or vague primary outcome
- P-values without effect estimates
- Conclusions that do not follow from the results
- Claims of causation from observational data without hedging
- Poor English that impedes understanding (many journals now offer language services and are more forgiving, but clarity is always essential)

## Common Mistakes

### 1. Abstract-Article Discrepancy
Numbers, percentages, or conclusions differ between the abstract and the full paper. This happens when the abstract is not updated after revisions to the manuscript. Always finalize the abstract LAST.

### 2. Including Data Not in the Article
Every number in the abstract must be traceable to the Results section. Reviewers check this.

### 3. Omitting Effect Estimates
Reporting "there was a significant difference (p < 0.05)" without stating the magnitude or direction of the difference. Always include: effect estimate (RR, OR, HR, mean difference), 95% CI, and p-value.

### 4. Burying the Primary Outcome
The primary outcome should be the FIRST result presented. Secondary findings should follow in order of importance, not in the order they were analyzed.

### 5. Vague Objectives
"We aimed to study the relationship between X and Y" is too vague. Specify the direction, population, and nature of the inquiry: "We aimed to determine whether X reduces the risk of Y in patients with Z."

### 6. Excessive Background
Three sentences of background in a 250-word abstract consume 20% of the space. One to two sentences is usually sufficient. Get to the objective quickly.

### 7. Multiple Objectives
An abstract should address one primary question. Multiple aims dilute the message and confuse readers about the study's focus.

## Conference Abstracts

### Differences from Journal Abstracts
- Often the only published record of the research (proceedings)
- May present preliminary data (pilot studies, interim analyses)
- Stricter word limits (often 250 words with no flexibility)
- Tables and figures may not be permitted (or limited to one small table)
- Often reviewed by committee with scoring rubrics

### Scoring Criteria (Typical)
- **Scientific quality**: Rigor of design and analysis
- **Originality**: Novel contribution to the field
- **Clinical relevance**: Impact on patient care
- **Clarity**: Writing quality and logical flow
- **Completeness**: All essential elements present

### Tips for Conference Abstract Success
- Front-load impact: Reviewers may score dozens in a sitting — make the significance immediately clear
- Include actual results (not "data will be presented" — this is increasingly rejected)
- Match the conference theme and audience
- Follow format instructions exactly (reviewers reject on formatting violations)
- Submit early (last-minute submissions may receive less favorable review slots)

## Abstract Checklist

Before submission, verify:
- [ ] Objective is specific, clear, and matches the identified gap
- [ ] Study design is explicitly named
- [ ] Setting and population are described
- [ ] Primary outcome is defined and reported first in Results
- [ ] Effect estimates include absolute numbers, relative measures, CIs, and p-values
- [ ] Conclusion directly answers the stated objective
- [ ] Conclusion is supported by the presented data (not overstated)
- [ ] No data in the abstract that are not in the full paper
- [ ] Word count is within the journal's limit
- [ ] Keywords are appropriate MeSH terms
- [ ] Trial registration number included (if applicable)
- [ ] Spelling, grammar, and formatting are correct
