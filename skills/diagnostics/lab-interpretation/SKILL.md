# Lab Interpretation

## Purpose

Provide structured, clinically actionable interpretation of common laboratory panels. Move beyond "normal vs abnormal" toward pattern recognition, differential generation, and rational reflex testing.

## Core Principles

1. **Never interpret a single value in isolation.** Labs form constellations; patterns matter more than individual numbers.
2. **Always correlate with clinical context.** A hemoglobin of 10 g/dL means different things in a 25-year-old menstruating woman vs a 70-year-old man with melena.
3. **Consider pre-analytical variables.** Hemolysis, lipemia, sample timing, tourniquet time, and tube type all affect results.
4. **Know your reference ranges.** They vary by lab, age, sex, pregnancy, and altitude.

## Complete Blood Count (CBC)

### Red Cell Parameters
- **Hemoglobin/Hematocrit**: Define anemia (WHO: Hb <13 g/dL men, <12 g/dL women). Hematocrit roughly 3x hemoglobin.
- **MCV**: Microcytic (<80 fL), normocytic (80-100 fL), macrocytic (>100 fL). Drives the anemia differential.
- **RDW**: Elevated (>14.5%) suggests mixed populations or early nutritional deficiency. Iron deficiency typically has high RDW; thalassemia trait has normal RDW.
- **Reticulocyte count**: Distinguishes hypo- vs hyperproliferative anemia. Correct for anemia: Reticulocyte Production Index (RPI) = retic% x (patient Hct / normal Hct) / maturation factor.

### Anemia Workup Algorithm
1. Confirm anemia (Hb below reference for age/sex)
2. Check MCV to classify morphology
3. Microcytic: ferritin (iron deficiency vs thalassemia vs chronic disease vs sideroblastic)
4. Normocytic: reticulocyte count (hemolysis/bleeding vs underproduction)
5. Macrocytic: B12/folate, reticulocyte count, peripheral smear (megaloblastic vs non-megaloblastic)

### White Cell Parameters
- **Leukocytosis** (>11,000/uL): Infection, inflammation, stress, steroids, malignancy, smoking
- **Leukopenia** (<4,000/uL): Viral infection, drug effect, autoimmune, bone marrow failure
- **Differential matters**: Neutrophilia (bacterial), lymphocytosis (viral, CLL), eosinophilia (allergy, parasites, drugs), monocytosis (chronic inflammation, recovery)
- **Left shift**: Increased bands (>6%) suggests acute bacterial infection or severe stress
- **Neutropenia grading**: Mild 1000-1500, moderate 500-1000, severe <500 (infection risk escalates)

### Platelet Parameters
- **Thrombocytopenia** (<150,000/uL): Decreased production (marrow), increased destruction (ITP, TTP/HUS, DIC, HIT), sequestration (splenomegaly), pseudothrombocytopenia (EDTA clumping — always check smear)
- **Thrombocytosis** (>400,000/uL): Reactive (iron deficiency, inflammation, infection) vs clonal (myeloproliferative neoplasms)

## Basic/Comprehensive Metabolic Panel (BMP/CMP)

### Electrolytes
- **Sodium**: Hyponatremia — assess volume status first (hypovolemic, euvolemic, hypervolemic). Check serum osmolality (true vs pseudo). SIADH criteria: euvolemic, low serum osm, inappropriately concentrated urine (>100 mOsm), urine Na >40.
- **Potassium**: Hypokalemia — check magnesium (must replete Mg to correct K). Hyperkalemia — rule out hemolysis artifact, check ECG immediately if >6.0 mEq/L.
- **Calcium**: Always correct for albumin: corrected Ca = measured Ca + 0.8 x (4.0 - albumin). If abnormal, check ionized calcium, PTH, 25-OH vitamin D, phosphorus.
- **Magnesium**: Often under-ordered. Check in refractory hypokalemia, hypocalcemia, arrhythmias, seizures.

### Renal Function
- **Creatinine**: Influenced by muscle mass, diet, medications (trimethoprim, cimetidine block tubular secretion). Estimate GFR with CKD-EPI equation, not Cockcroft-Gault for staging.
- **BUN**: BUN:Cr ratio >20:1 suggests prerenal azotemia or upper GI bleeding.
- **BUN and Cr both rising**: Distinguish prerenal (reversible with fluids) vs intrinsic renal (ATN — fractional excretion of sodium >2%) vs postrenal (obstruction — get ultrasound).

### Hepatic Panel (CMP)
- **AST/ALT**: Hepatocellular pattern. ALT more liver-specific. AST:ALT >2:1 suggests alcoholic liver disease. Massive elevation (>1000) — viral hepatitis, ischemic hepatitis, drug toxicity (acetaminophen).
- **Alkaline phosphatase**: Cholestatic pattern. Confirm hepatic origin with GGT (bone ALP is GGT-negative).
- **Bilirubin**: Direct (conjugated) vs indirect (unconjugated). Isolated indirect hyperbilirubinemia — hemolysis or Gilbert syndrome. Direct hyperbilirubinemia — hepatobiliary obstruction or hepatocellular dysfunction.
- **Albumin**: Marker of synthetic function and nutritional status. Half-life ~20 days — not useful for acute changes.

## Coagulation Studies

- **PT/INR**: Extrinsic pathway (factors VII, X, V, II, fibrinogen). Monitors warfarin. Elevated in liver disease, vitamin K deficiency, DIC.
- **aPTT**: Intrinsic pathway (factors XII, XI, IX, VIII, X, V, II, fibrinogen). Monitors unfractionated heparin. Elevated in hemophilia A (VIII) and B (IX), lupus anticoagulant (paradoxically causes thrombosis).
- **Mixing study**: If prolonged PT/aPTT corrects with 1:1 mixing — factor deficiency. If it does not correct — inhibitor present.
- **Fibrinogen**: Acute phase reactant (rises in inflammation). Low in DIC, severe liver disease, massive transfusion.
- **D-dimer**: Fibrin degradation product. High sensitivity, low specificity. Useful to rule OUT PE/DVT (high NPV). Age-adjusted cutoff: age x 10 ug/L for patients >50 years.

## Cardiac Markers

- **Troponin (high-sensitivity)**: Gold standard for myocardial injury. Rise within 1-3 hours. Use 0/1h or 0/3h rule-in/rule-out algorithms per ESC guidelines. Remember: troponin elevation ≠ MI. Type 2 MI (supply-demand mismatch), myocarditis, PE, renal failure, sepsis all elevate troponin.
- **BNP/NT-proBNP**: Heart failure biomarkers. BNP <100 pg/mL or NT-proBNP <300 pg/mL effectively rules out acute HF. Elevated in renal failure, AF, PE, sepsis. NT-proBNP is not cleared by neprilysin — preferred if patient on sacubitril/valsartan.

## Thyroid Function

- **TSH-first strategy**: TSH is the most sensitive screening test for thyroid dysfunction.
- **TSH high, fT4 low**: Primary hypothyroidism
- **TSH low, fT4 high**: Primary hyperthyroidism (next: T3, TSH receptor antibodies, thyroid uptake scan)
- **TSH low, fT4 normal**: Subclinical hyperthyroidism or sick euthyroid (check clinical context)
- **TSH normal-low, fT4 low**: Central hypothyroidism (pituitary) — do not rely on TSH alone
- **Sick euthyroid (non-thyroidal illness)**: Low T3, variable TSH — avoid testing thyroid function in acutely ill patients unless strong clinical suspicion

## Inflammatory Markers

- **CRP**: Non-specific. Rises within 6-8 hours, peaks 48 hours. Useful for monitoring treatment response. hs-CRP for cardiovascular risk stratification.
- **Procalcitonin (PCT)**: More specific for bacterial infection than CRP. PCT <0.25 ng/mL — bacterial infection unlikely (consider stopping/withholding antibiotics in lower respiratory infections). PCT >0.5 ng/mL — bacterial infection likely. Useful for antibiotic stewardship.
- **ESR**: Very non-specific. Rises slowly. Elevated in infection, inflammation, malignancy, pregnancy, anemia. Temporal arteritis (ESR >50 mm/hr with clinical features) and polymyalgia rheumatica are classic indications.

## Urinalysis

- **Dipstick**: pH, specific gravity, protein, glucose, ketones, blood, leukocyte esterase, nitrites, bilirubin, urobilinogen
- **Leukocyte esterase + nitrites**: Together have high specificity for UTI. Nitrites only positive with nitrate-reducing organisms (Enterobacteriaceae — not Enterococcus, Pseudomonas, or Staphylococcus).
- **Proteinuria**: Dipstick detects albumin only. Quantify with spot urine albumin:creatinine ratio (ACR) or protein:creatinine ratio (PCR). ACR >30 mg/g = albuminuria.
- **Microscopy**: RBC casts (glomerulonephritis), WBC casts (pyelonephritis/interstitial nephritis), muddy brown granular casts (ATN), hyaline casts (nonspecific), waxy/broad casts (CKD)

## Interpretation Framework

When presented with lab results, follow this sequence:
1. **Flag critical values** requiring immediate action (K >6.5, Na <120, glucose <40, troponin elevation, lactate >4)
2. **Identify patterns** across related tests (hepatocellular vs cholestatic, DIC triad, tumor lysis)
3. **Correlate with clinical picture** (pre-test probability changes interpretation)
4. **Recommend reflex tests** (e.g., iron studies for microcytic anemia, peripheral smear for unexplained cytopenias)
5. **Suggest monitoring interval** based on trajectory and clinical stability

## Common Pitfalls

- Treating the number, not the patient
- Ignoring hemolysis flags (falsely elevates K, LDH, AST)
- Forgetting albumin correction for calcium
- Ordering thyroid function tests in acutely ill patients
- Using D-dimer in high pre-test probability patients (order imaging directly)
- Interpreting a single troponin without serial measurement or clinical context
